Why GreenTrack exists

One batch, four stops, zero guesswork

GreenTrack started as a way for growers to track their own plots. It became something bigger once we realised the same record could follow the harvest all the way to the plate.

Our story

Built from a simple frustration

It started with a grower who kept a paper notebook for every plot — planting dates, watering, which bed got which pesticide — and a shopper two towns away staring at a label that just said "fresh," with no way to check. GreenTrack closes that gap: one record, started the day a seed goes into the ground, that keeps collecting stamps until it reaches whoever's about to eat it.

GreenTrack functions as an end-to-end digital farm logbook and verification platform. For growers, it's a smart mobile diary that follows a crop through its entire lifecycle — GPS-pinned plots, logged irrigation, weather-matched schedules, and enforced safety countdowns on pest treatments — before aggregating that history into a single tamper-proof QR code at harvest. For chefs and diners on the other end, that same code unlocks the exact origin, harvest timestamp, farm-level weather history, organic verification, and a precise nutritional breakdown, in the time it takes to scan a label.

The problem

Three gaps in the food chain, one record to close them

Modern supply chains run on basic inventory software. That leaves a structural, three-fold gap between what actually happens on a plot and what a buyer ever gets to see.

Gap A — The anonymity black hole

Once a smallholder's premium, sustainably grown produce is sold to a bulk distributor, it gets mixed into generic crates with lower-grade stock. There's no way left to prove whose harvest it actually was, so the grower can't brand it, and can't command a premium price for it.

Gap B — No verified data at the source

Most food-tracking systems only switch on once produce reaches a packaging facility. Nobody can prove what happened in the soil — whether the water was clean, the crop survived a storm, or a pesticide was given its mandatory days to break down before harvest.

Gap C — Skepticism and nutritional blindness

Today's shoppers and diners distrust generic "organic" or "fresh" labels, and rightly so — they're easily faked. Faced with a plate or a shelf, they have no fast way to check real freshness or pull up an accurate nutritional breakdown for what's in front of them.

Why GreenTrack wins

GreenTrack closes all three gaps at once by creating a two-sided, data-driven trust loop: it hands the grower a digital voice and climate-smart warnings, while giving everyone downstream unalterable proof of safety, nutrition, and origin — in under two seconds, one scan at a time.

Field notes

What "STOP 01" looks like from Mwangi's plot

Every new planting starts as a new crop batch: crop name, farming method, and a GPS pin dropped straight onto the plot. From there Mwangi runs his watering through the same app — logging the water source and the time of every irrigation so the next round schedules itself, instead of relying on memory or guesswork.

Weather stops being folklore, too. Rather than leaning on farming patterns passed down through generations, GreenTrack tells him when the long and short rains are coming and how much water each crop needs through them. If pests show up, he photographs the affected leaves and the app's pest-diagnosis module matches the symptoms to a treatment and dose — no panic, no guessing.

Harvest isn't just "when it looks ready." A pre-harvest interval, tied to whatever pesticide was last applied, locks the harvest button until any spray residue has fully cleared. A day or two out, Mwangi logs an estimated yield so partnered buyers know a batch is coming before it's even picked — cutting the food waste that piles up when produce just sits around. When he finally taps "Log Harvest," the app timestamps it, pulls in the ambient weather, and rolls every logged step into one record. The final verified weight generates a unique QR code, printed straight onto the crates — and Mwangi moves on to planting the next batch.

Where it happens

Every stamp starts on the grower's phone

Plots, irrigation, pest checks, and the harvest lock all live in the same app a grower already opens every morning — the traceability is a side effect of doing their normal work.
